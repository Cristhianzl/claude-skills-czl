# PR-FAQ (Amazon Working Backwards)

Purpose: the Amazon Working Backwards PR-FAQ template and when to reach for it instead of a PRD.

## When to use

Use a PR-FAQ for **strategic or ambiguous bets** where desirability and viability are not yet proven — before committing engineering to a full PRD. You write the launch announcement *first*, working backwards from the customer, to force clarity on the value before any building. Keep the press release to roughly **one page**, and **iterate** it — early drafts are meant to be torn apart in review. If the PR can't be written compellingly, the idea isn't ready.

## Part 1 — the press release (~1 page)

Written as if the product already shipped, in plain customer language (no jargon, no internal metrics).

- **Heading** — the product name and a one-line description the target customer would understand.
- **Subheading** — one sentence: who the customer is and the core benefit they get.
- **Summary paragraph** — what it is and the headline benefit, as if announcing it today.
- **Problem paragraph** — the customer problem this solves, stated from the customer's point of view.
- **Solution paragraph** — how the product solves that problem, simply.
- **Company quote** — a leader explaining why the company built this.
- **Customer quote** — a hypothetical customer describing the benefit in their own words.
- **How to get started** — the call to action; how a customer begins using it.

## Part 2 — the FAQ

The FAQ is where the hard questions live — the ones the one-page PR deliberately omits.

**External FAQs (customer / media):** the questions a customer or journalist would ask — pricing, availability, how it works, what's different from alternatives, privacy.

**Internal FAQs (the team — finance, ops, legal, product):**

- **TAM / demand** — how big is the market and what's the evidence anyone wants this?
- **Economics & path to profitability** — unit economics, cost to build and run, when and how it pays back.
- **Dependencies** — what teams, technologies, partners, or data this requires.
- **Risks** — what could make this fail (adoption, technical, legal, competitive) and how you'd know early.
- **Success conditions** — what must be true for this to be considered a success; the metrics and thresholds.
